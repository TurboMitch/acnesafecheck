Is PEG-16 Lanolin comedogenic?

Quick answer: PEG-16 Lanolin has a comedogenicity rating of 4/5 — high pore-clogging risk. It is not a common fungal-acne trigger.

4/5
High pore-clogging risk
Comedogenicity scale: 0 = won't clog pores, 5 = very likely to clog pores.

PEG-16 Lanolin sits high on the comedogenic scale. For acne-prone or breakout-prone skin it is one of the ingredients most likely to block pores and contribute to blackheads, whiteheads and pimples, especially when it appears near the top of an ingredient list (higher concentration).

What is PEG-16 Lanolin?

PEG-16 Lanolin is an emulsifier that keeps oil and water mixed in a formula. On an ingredient label it may also appear as peg 16 lanolin. You'll most often see it in creams, lotions and any product that blends oil and water.

Fungal-acne (Malassezia) safe? Yes — it is not on common Malassezia (fungal acne) trigger lists. Check a whole product for fungal-acne triggers →

Does PEG-16 Lanolin clog pores?

Yes — PEG-16 Lanolin is one of the higher pore-clogging ingredients (rated 4/5) and is best avoided in leave-on products if you are acne-prone. Remember that where an ingredient sits in the list matters: PEG-16 Lanolin near the top means a higher concentration and more impact than the same ingredient near the end.

Acne-safe alternatives to PEG-16 Lanolin

If PEG-16 Lanolin breaks you out, these lower-rated emulsifier options are far less likely to clog pores:

  • Hydrogenated Lecithin — Low risk · 1/5
  • PEG-100 Stearate — Low risk · 1/5
  • PEG-150 Distearate — Low risk · 1/5
  • PEG-40 Stearate — Low risk · 1/5
  • Polysorbate 80 — Low risk · 1/5

How to read this rating

A comedogenicity rating is general guidance compiled from published reference lists, not a lab result for a specific formula. An ingredient near the end of an ingredient list is present in a small amount, so its rating matters less than one near the top. Read our full sources & methodology.
