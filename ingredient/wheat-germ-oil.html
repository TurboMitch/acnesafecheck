Is Wheat Germ Oil comedogenic?

Quick answer: Wheat Germ Oil has a comedogenicity rating of 5/5 — high pore-clogging risk. It is also a common fungal-acne (Malassezia) trigger.

5/5
High pore-clogging risk
Comedogenicity scale: 0 = won't clog pores, 5 = very likely to clog pores.

Wheat Germ Oil sits high on the comedogenic scale. For acne-prone or breakout-prone skin it is one of the ingredients most likely to block pores and contribute to blackheads, whiteheads and pimples, especially when it appears near the top of an ingredient list (higher concentration).

What is Wheat Germ Oil?

Wheat Germ Oil is a plant or carrier oil used to moisturise and condition skin. On an ingredient label it may also appear as triticum vulgare germ oil. You'll most often see it in facial oils, cleansing oils, balms, lip products and richer moisturisers.

Fungal-acne (Malassezia) safe? No — this is a common Malassezia (fungal acne) trigger, so avoid it if you have fungal acne. Check a whole product for fungal-acne triggers →

Does Wheat Germ Oil clog pores?

Yes — Wheat Germ Oil is one of the higher pore-clogging ingredients (rated 5/5) and is best avoided in leave-on products if you are acne-prone. Remember that where an ingredient sits in the list matters: Wheat Germ Oil near the top means a higher concentration and more impact than the same ingredient near the end.

How we rate comedogenicity — and its limits

The 0–5 comedogenicity scale comes from dermatology research that measured how much individual ingredients clogged pores, originally using the rabbit-ear assay (Fulton et al., 1984). A rating reflects an ingredient's potential to clog pores at high concentration — not a guarantee that any finished product will break you out.

That distinction matters. A later review (Draelos & DiNardo, 2006) found that a single ingredient's comedogenic score does not reliably predict how a complete formula behaves on real skin: concentration, the rest of the formula, and your own skin all change the result. So read Wheat Germ Oil's 5/5 as a risk signal — most useful when the ingredient sits near the top of the list — rather than a verdict on one specific product.
